Evaluating the Effectiveness of Zero Trust Architecture in Preventing Cloud-Based Breaches in Healthcare Systems

Significance/Contribution to the Discipline/Research Problem

The healthcare sector’s transition to cloud-based infrastructure has introduced unprecedented efficiency but also elevated cybersecurity risks. High-profile ransomware incidents and API-related breaches have underscored vulnerabilities inherent in traditional perimeter-based security. As healthcare systems process sensitive personal and clinical data, breaches pose dual threats to operational continuity and patient trust. Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) has emerged as a transformative framework promising enhanced breach resistance by rejecting implicit trust and enforcing continuous authentication, segmentation, and least privilege access.

This study addresses a critical knowledge gap by evaluating ZTA’s effectiveness specifically within healthcare cloud environments. While theoretical and conceptual models abound, empirical studies measuring real-world impact across diverse healthcare settings remain limited. By interrogating the operational and behavioural outcomes of ZTA adoption, this project contributes valuable insight for IT security governance, healthcare management, and cybersecurity policy.

Research Question

How effective is Zero Trust Architecture in preventing cloud-based breaches in healthcare systems?

Aims and Objectives

  • Assess the practical outcomes of ZTA implementation in healthcare organisations with cloud-based systems.
  • Identify success factors, limitations, and barriers in ZTA adoption.
  • Evaluate how ZTA mitigates ransomware, insider threats, and API/IoT vulnerabilities in healthcare settings.
  • Provide evidence-based recommendations for policymakers and IT leaders in healthcare cybersecurity.

Key Literature Related to the Project

Zero Trust: Applications, Challenges, and Opportunities outlines ZTA as a strategy rooted in micro-segmentation, continuous identity verification, and contextual access controls. Ewoh and Vartiainen (2024) stress the socio-technical vulnerabilities that ZTA aims to resolve, such as insider misuse and poor access offboarding. Case-based research by Ewoh, Vartiainen, and Mantere (2025) suggests that effective ZTA deployment must combine technical tools with human governance. However, longitudinal data and cross-context evaluations are lacking. The review by Kruse et al. (2017) confirms encryption and IAM as foundational, but acknowledges that traditional controls alone are insufficient in modern cloud ecosystems.

Methodology/Development Strategy/Research Design

A mixed-methods approach will be employed:

  • Quantitative data will be collected through structured surveys distributed to healthcare IT departments in ZTA-adopting institutions, focusing on breach incidence, incident response time, and downtime before and after implementation.
  • Qualitative insights will be gathered via semi-structured interviews with cybersecurity professionals and clinical IT staff to explore organisational culture, training, and perceived ZTA efficacy.
  • Literature analysis will supplement empirical findings by reviewing peer-reviewed case studies and industry reports on ZTA in cloud healthcare contexts.

Data will be triangulated to ensure validity and comprehensiveness.

Ethical Considerations and Risk Assessment

Participants will provide informed consent, and data will be anonymised to protect organisational confidentiality. Ethical approval will be sought through the relevant institutional review board. Data will be securely stored, and participation will be voluntary with withdrawal rights respected. The study carries low ethical risk but will mitigate potential reputational sensitivities via pseudonymisation and secure data handling protocols.

Description of Artefact

A visual framework illustrating the components and outcomes of effective ZTA implementation in healthcare will be developed. This artefact will support knowledge translation for technical and non-technical stakeholders.
