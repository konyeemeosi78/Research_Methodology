Cloud Security Risks in the Healthcare Sector

Presented by: Onyeemeosi Kingsley Oke

Programme: MSc Cyber Security, University of Essex

Module: Research Methodology

Introduction

Cloud computing has emerged as a transformative force in healthcare, revolutionizing data storage, management, and service delivery. With the rise of digitized health records, telemedicine, and interconnected diagnostic tools, cloud services have enabled healthcare institutions to transcend traditional infrastructure limitations. Hybrid cloud architectures, integrating both public and private cloud infrastructures, offer flexibility, cost-efficiency, and scalability while retaining control over sensitive data.

However, these benefits come with notable cybersecurity challenges. Complex environments, inconsistent access controls, fragmented monitoring protocols, and heterogeneous device networks increase vulnerabilities. Healthcare data’s sensitivity and black-market value make it a high-value target for cybercriminals. This study investigates these vulnerabilities, assesses mitigation strategies, and explores regulatory, operational, and ethical implications.

Background

The reliance on digital platforms in healthcare has elevated the importance of secure systems. Cloud computing offers on-demand resources, scalable storage, and cost-effective infrastructure but introduces security challenges in hybrid or multi-cloud deployments. Legacy systems, strained IT budgets, and incidents like the WannaCry ransomware attack on the NHS highlight the sector’s cyber preparedness gaps.

Threats extend beyond ransomware to include insider threats, misconfigurations, and risks from IoT devices and third-party platforms. Traditional perimeter defenses are inadequate, and fragmented logging in hybrid clouds complicates incident detection and response.

Research Problem

Despite cybersecurity advancements, cloud environments in healthcare experience frequent breaches due to technical, systemic, and regulatory issues. Conventional tools like firewalls are insufficient for dynamic cloud systems. This research examines the interplay of technological, operational, and ethical risks in healthcare cloud security.

Significance and Contribution

Cloud adoption in healthcare is constrained by regulations like HIPAA and GDPR, which don’t fully address clinical, operational, and ethical realities. This study offers a holistic, healthcare-specific understanding of cloud security risks, addressing usability, trust, and ethical concerns like consent and data sovereignty.

Research Question

What are the key security risks associated with cloud computing in healthcare, and how can these risks be mitigated through context-sensitive, ethically sound strategies?

Research Aims and Objectives

This study aims to evaluate security risks in healthcare cloud systems, particularly hybrid environments, by:

  • Identifying common vulnerabilities.
  • Assessing mitigation strategy effectiveness.
  • Exploring ethical and regulatory factors.
  • Analyzing real-world incidents and stakeholder perspectives.

Literature Review

Hybrid cloud security faces challenges like IAM gaps, ransomware exploiting unpatched systems, and supply chain attacks. Regulations lag behind innovation, and behavioral tools raise privacy concerns. Effective, ethical security models are needed to address these issues.

Research Methodology

A secondary qualitative study analyzes three UK healthcare cases using purposive sampling. Data sources include interviews, reports, and policies. Thematic analysis follows Braun and Clarke’s (2006) approach, with triangulation, member checking, and NHS ethical standards ensuring rigour.

Ethics and Risk

Ethics adhere to the Declaration of Helsinki and UK data laws. Behavioral monitoring risks are mitigated with anonymous feedback and third-party audits. Sandboxing and fail-safes reduce disruptions.

Description of Artefacts

The study produces three artefacts:

  • Risk Taxonomy: Summarizes cloud vulnerabilities, classified by severity, vector, and mitigation difficulty.
  • Qualitative Evaluation Framework: Blends technical, ethical, and operational criteria.
  • Policy Guidance Document: Offers recommendations on governance, regulation, training, and ethical surveillance.

Proposed Timeline

The research spans eight months:

  • Month 1: Finalize literature review and ethical considerations.
  • Months 2-3: Data analysis.
  • Months 4-6: Artefact development.
  • Month 7: Artefact validation.
  • Month 8: Dissertation compilation and revisions.

Conclusion

Cloud computing offers opportunities and risks in healthcare. This study synthesizes secondary data to provide adaptable, ethically grounded strategies for secure cloud adoption, contributing to trust-based digital infrastructures.
