Literature Review: Cloud Security Risks in the Healthcare Sector

Cloud Deployment Models in Healthcare

Healthcare institutions adopt three main cloud deployment models—public, private, and hybrid—each offering unique advantages and challenges (Quezada-Mendoza et al., 2019). Public clouds, such as AWS and Azure, provide scalable, cost-effective infrastructure suitable for smaller clinics with limited in-house resources. Private clouds offer controlled environments with enhanced data governance, favoured by large hospital networks requiring strict compliance and system isolation. Hybrid clouds blend both approaches, enabling data and workload portability between public and private environments.

Ewoh & Vartiainen (2024) caution that hybrid models, while offering flexibility, complicate data monitoring and incident response. Coordination between disparate security models—public cloud IAM, private infrastructure logging, and third-party BAA agreements—can increase integration risk and configuration errors. Traditional literature often portrays hybrid clouds as ideal; more recent reviews emphasise the operational complexity and elevated integration demands inherent to hybrid environments.

Moreover, the adoption of hybrid cloud necessitates uniform security policy orchestration across both public and private components. Tools such as cloud access security brokers (CASBs), unified logging platforms, and cross-domain encryption must be deployed to prevent misconfigurations and enforcement gaps. Public and private clouds require synchronised policy updates to avoid security drift.

Hybrid models also expose broader internal and external threats; misconfigured network routes or identity federation vulnerabilities in one segment can have cascading effects. In some regions, national regulations mandate data localization, further complicating hybrid architectures. Consequently, cloud governance strategies must account for regulatory, technical, and operational overlap, transitioning from model-specific policies to workflow-driven frameworks that ensure consistent protection across hybrid ecosystems.

Data Breaches & Ransomware

Data breaches and ransomware remain the most destructive cyber threats to cloud-centric healthcare operations. Between 2017 and 2020, healthcare organisations incurred average breach-related costs of $7.6 million (Lawyer & Corina, 2017), with collateral damage often spanning legal liabilities, reputational harm, and patient trust erosion. Lawyer & Corina (2017a) highlight how the WannaCry attack in 2017 left 80 NHS hospitals crippled, delaying surgeries and diagnoses as an early sign of ransomware’s potential to threaten patient safety.

More recent incidents reflect evolving attacker tactics. In 2021, the Irish Health Service Executive (HSE) was compromised by Conti ransomware, severely impacting network functionality; inadequate segmentation, lack of multifactor authentication, and lax patch schedules were cited as contributing vulnerabilities (Journal of Medical Internet Research, n.d. a). Later, in 2024, Change Healthcare, a major U.S. claims-processing entity, suffered a cyber disruption affecting nearly all hospital billing and EHR platforms, emphasising cloud supply chain risk (Journal of Medical Internet Research, n.d. a).

Ewoh et al. (2025) advanced the concept of attacker-centric threat modelling, which encourages analysing adversarial behaviours, economic motivations, and chain-of-compromise mechanisms within cloud ecosystems. This methodology contrasts older vulnerability-only frameworks by advocating a threat-informed proactive stance aimed at predicting attack vectors and defending accordingly.

Recent ransomware variants also employ double-extortion, threatening to leak patient data if ransom is not paid. This creates dual risk: patient privacy violations alongside system unavailability. Advanced mitigation strategies now include red-teaming, regular data backups isolated from primary networks, cybersecurity insurer readiness exercises, and public-private threat-sharing agreements.

Blockchain-based integrity ledgers are being explored to detect tampering via immutable logs, but adoption remains limited due to operational complexity and resistance. National cybersecurity advisories now require threat intelligence integration with incident response planning. These frameworks advocate continuous risk assessment, enhanced logging, threat hunting, and offensive cyber techniques to disrupt attacker infrastructure.

Insider Threats & Sociotechnical Vulnerabilities

Quezada-Mendoza et al. (2019) highlight that insider threats—whether malicious or inadvertent—pose significant risks in healthcare. Such threats have been implicated in 60% of healthcare data breaches. Healthcare cloud environments typically involve multiple user roles, external contractors, and endpoint devices, which complicates access control and oversight.

Zero-Trust models address these challenges by eliminating the implicit inside-out trust boundary ((PDF) Zero Trust Security, 2025). Ghasemshirazi et al. (2023) recommend continuous identity verification, device posture checks, micro-segmentation, and adaptive access decisions based on risk scoring, effectively minimizing lateral movement. Contextual authentication, repeated permission validation, and behavioural analytics can detect anomalies such as unusual login patterns or sudden access requests.

However, technology alone is insufficient. Ewoh & Vartiainen’s (2024) systematic review revealed that most insider breaches are rooted in unclear policies, insufficient training, weak offboarding protocols, and inability to correlate human factors with technical controls. They argue for a sociotechnical systems approach that integrates human resource processes, coaching, accountability frameworks, and change management with robust technical safeguards.

Examples of successful applications include combining IAM tools with regular digital hygiene training, simulated phishing campaigns, quarterly access window approvals, and automated user deactivation upon employment changes. Technical interventions are also linked to behavioural nudges like just-in-time warnings, session timeouts, and forced reauthentication during sensitive operations. Cultivating a security-centric organisational culture is imperative.

Applications such as segmentation of data access into need-to-know zones and logging every user action are examples of combining human oversight with technical instrumentation. However, these systems can face resistance if perceived as invasive or excessively complex, underscoring the need to involve users in design and training to build trust and foster adoption.

API and IoT Vulnerabilities: The Expanding Attack Surface

Healthcare depends extensively on APIs to connect EHRs, imaging solutions, lab systems, billing platforms, and patient portals. Yet insecure APIs have been a recurring vulnerability. A 2020 breach exposing 500,000 patient records occurred when weak authentication and lack of encryption allowed unauthorized access to a medical scheduling API (Quezada-Mendoza et al., 2019). IoT devices like wearables, infusion pumps, and remote monitors also broaden attack vectors through unencrypted telemetry and firmware weaknesses.

Micro-segmentation—dividing API zones into secure, role-based compartments—has emerged as a key defense. Ghasemshirazi et al. (2023-b) describe how zero-trust micro-segmentation drastically narrows the blast radius of a breach while maintaining necessary interoperability. Encryption via TLS, OAuth 2.0 for authentication, continuous penetration testing, and secure coding practices are explicitly recommended.

IoT and device security need hardware-level validation like secure boot, signed firmware updates, and communication peers tightly restricted via certificate-based authentication. Device inventories are essential to identify unmanaged or orphaned endpoints that could facilitate lateral movement or denial-of-service attacks.

A major challenge lies in the rarity of long-term API governance monitoring. Even when tools are available, healthcare organisations often deprioritise API testing and inventory due to cost and operational complexity. AI-driven API discovery and monitoring tools with dashboards and analytics can help uncover shadow APIs and detect anomalies before misuse.

Denial-of-Service (DoS) Threats to Care Continuity

Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks, particularly Distributed DoS (DDoS) attacks, pose significant disruption to cloud-based healthcare services. A 2019 attack on a German hospital shut down access to critical systems, delaying patient treatment and emergency interventions (Lawyer & Corina, 2017a). The Journal of Medical Internet Research (n.d. a) reports strong correlations between service outages and negative clinical outcomes, including increased error rates and extended hospitalization lengths.

Mitigation strategies include deploying Web Application Firewalls, using auto-scaling cloud platforms, and integrating AI-enabled traffic analysis. Selecting geographically diversified cloud nodes further limits the impact of localized attacks. Simulated DoS scenarios should be included in standard emergency drills alongside ransomware and insider threat exercises to ensure preparedness and continuity.

However, academic literature lacks longitudinal studies measuring the efficacy of these measures across cloud environments over multiple years. Metrics such as downtime reduction, incident detection time, and staff response are rarely tracked in published case studies. As cloud systems evolve, better metrics and measurement strategies are essential.

Compliance, Legal & Ethical Risks

Healthcare data security in the cloud intersects heavily with regulation. HIPAA in the U.S., GDPR in Europe, and various local regulations demand encryption, access controls, breach notification, and audit logs. Violations can result in penalties up to $1.5 million (HIPAA) or €20 million (GDPR) (Isabalija & Kituyi, 2017). However, Lawyer & Corina (2017b) report that many cloud providers still fall short of regulatory encryption and logging expectations, exposing healthcare organisations to legal risk.

BAAs (Business Associate Agreements) define accountability for cloud vendors, but post-pandemic supply chain attacks (like Change Healthcare) forced re-evaluation of shared liability and audit mechanisms. The Journal of Medical Internet Research (2024-b) emphasises that emerging AI systems bring new governance complexities—algorithmic bias, model interpretability, data provenance, and accountability—which must be addressed in regulatory updates.

Ethical concerns extend beyond legal compliance to dignity and patient autonomy. Health data is inherently personal, and its protection impacts public trust and equity. Ethics committees now incorporate privacy-by-design, data minimisation, and consent frameworks into cloud systems. Emerging academic studies also call for transparency protocols—patients should be notified how their data is used in AI diagnostics or public health research.

Mitigation Strategies: Integrating Technical, Human & Governance Elements

Encryption & Key Management

AES-256 remains the benchmark for encrypting data at rest and in transit (Kruse et al., 2017). Key management must include Hardware Security Modules (HSMs), regular key rotations, and the use of separate encryption zones for highly sensitive data.

Identity & Access Management (IAM) and Zero Trust

RBAC combined with MFA is a baseline requirement. ZTA implements continual access verification, device posture, contextual authentication, micro-segmentation, and dynamic risk scoring (Ghasemshirazi et al., 2023-b). Access permissions are re-evaluated at every transaction.

Threat Intelligence & Behavioural Analytics

SIEM platforms should incorporate behavioural analytics, anomaly detection, and threat intelligence feeds. Ewoh et al. (2025) promote attacker-informed defense modelling that anticipates adversary techniques, enabling pre-emptive detection and containment.

Sociotechnical Measures

Ewoh & Vartiainen (2024) reveal that many breaches are rooted in organisational weaknesses. Solution packages include regular phishing training, scenario-based drills, policy feedback loops, change management processes, and incident logging.

Penetration Testing & Adversary Simulation

Red-teaming exercises covering APIs, IoT, legacy systems, and cloud configurations offer proactive risk identification. Blue team and purple team collaboration strengthens organizational defence through continual improvement cycles.

Standards & Certification

Formal frameworks such as ISO 27001 and NIST CSF (including SP 800-207 for Zero Trust) provide structured guidance. Compliance must be validated through operational audits and applied through real-world configurations, not just paperwork.

Cyber Insurance & Operational Resilience

Cyber readiness plans must incorporate scenario-testing, tabletop exercises, and coordinated response protocols between technical, clinical, and administrative teams. MSSPs provide monitoring and threat hunt capabilities, while insurers can underwrite response and recovery costs in complex incidents.

Ethical and Governance Dimensions

Healthcare organisations bear ethical obligations to preserve patient privacy, agency, and consent, extending beyond regulatory compliance. Under GDPR, breach notifications must occur within 72 hours; failure to do so, as witnessed in a 2018 U.S. hospital case, eroded public trust (Kruse et al., 2017).

Ethics committees increasingly engage with cloud procurement decisions to ensure privacy-by-design, data sovereignty, algorithmic fairness, and end-to-end consent management for AI applications. The Journal of Medical Internet Research (2024-b) encourages algorithmic transparency, auditability, and governance oversight committees for data-driven clinical tools.

Human-centred design principles guide usability and accessibility. Ewoh & Vartiainen (2024) suggest embedding privacy respect and communication standards into portal designs, ensuring patients understand their data’s journey and use. Health equity considerations mandate that cloud systems serve all demographics fairly, not perpetuating historical biases or exclusion.

Strengths, Limitations & Research Gaps

Strengths

• Integrates modern threat models: attack-centred defense, zero-trust, supply chain awareness.
• Includes case studies that emphasise patient outcomes.
• Combines technical, organisational, and ethical analysis for a comprehensive perspective.

Limitations

• Empirical studies on long-term ZTA implementation in clinical environments are limited.
• Comparative research across different healthcare systems, especially low-income or rural settings, remains sparse.
• Patient and clinician perspectives on data sharing and cloud trust have not been thoroughly investigated.

Research Recommendations

• Conduct longitudinal studies evaluating zero-trust architectures in health systems (operational impact, staff acceptance, cost-effectiveness).
• Compare cross-national approaches to cloud security law, technology adoption, and breach governance.
• Undertake qualitative studies capturing patient trust, usability, and perceived autonomy.
• Explore emerging threats—quantum-resistant encryption, AI-generated threats, and blockchain-based audit trails.

Conclusion

Cloud computing offers transformative benefits for healthcare—enhancing efficiency, collaboration, and patient care—but introduces multifaceted security, privacy, and legal risks. Ransomware, insider misuse, API misconfigurations, IoT vulnerabilities, and compliance breaches form a complex threat matrix. Through layered defence—combining encryption, Zero Trust, behavior analytics, operational readiness, governance, and ethical stewardship—health systems can leverage the cloud securely.

Healthcare organisations must prioritise investments in advanced architectures, staff training, patient engagement, and ethical frameworks. Cross-disciplinary coordination between clinicians, IT professionals, cybersecurity experts, policymakers, and patients is essential to ensure cloud innovation remains both secure and human-focused. By adopting agile defence models and embedding ethical values, cloud-powered healthcare can evolve into a trust-based, resilient, and patient-centred model fit for the future.
